There are books that sit politely on your shelf, and then there's *The Master and Margarita*, which climbs off the shelf at 3am, sets your kitchen on fire, and reads you poetry while it burns.

Bulgakov wrote this knowing it would never be published in his lifetime. You can feel that freedom on every page — the recklessness of someone writing without the possibility of consequence.

## The Devil Visits Moscow

The novel opens with Satan arriving in Soviet Moscow, accompanied by a retinue that includes a giant cat who walks on two legs and drinks vodka. What follows is a demolition of Soviet bureaucracy, literary pretension, and human cowardice disguised as a slapstick comedy.

> "Manuscripts don't burn."

That line alone is worth the price of admission.

## Two Stories in One

Interleaved with the Moscow chaos is the story of Pontius Pilate and Yeshua — told with a restraint and beauty that feels like it belongs in a different book entirely. The contrast is the point. Bulgakov is saying: *this is what literature can do when it's honest*.

## Why It Stays With Me

I think about the Master often. A writer who produces one perfect work and then can't survive the world's response to it. There's something painfully true in that, something Bulgakov understood from the inside.

Read it. Then read it again. You'll find different things the second time.
