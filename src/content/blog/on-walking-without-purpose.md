I've started walking without my phone. Not as some digital detox performance — I just got tired of reaching for it at every red light, every pause, every moment my brain might have had space to think.

The first few walks were agony. My hand kept drifting to my pocket. I'd catch myself composing tweets about the experience of not tweeting. The irony was not lost on me.

## What Happens After the Discomfort

Around the twenty-minute mark, something shifts. The internal monologue quiets. You start noticing things: the way light hits a particular wall at 4pm, the sound of a conversation in a language you don't speak, the geometry of a crack in the sidewalk.

These aren't profound observations. That's the point. Profundity is overrated. What matters is the *quality of attention* — the difference between looking and seeing.

## Walking as Thinking

The Greeks knew this. The Peripatetics didn't walk because they needed exercise; they walked because movement and thought are linked at some fundamental level. Your body moves forward and your mind follows.

I've solved more problems on a thirty-minute walk than in three hours at a desk. Not because walking is magical, but because it removes all the things that aren't thinking and replaces them with rhythm.

## The Argument for Purposelessness

We've optimised everything. Every walk is tracked, every route is mapped, every minute is accounted for. But the most interesting things happen in the gaps — in the unscheduled, the untracked, the purposeless.

Walk somewhere you don't need to go. See what you find.
